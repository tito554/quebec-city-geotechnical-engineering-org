In-Situ Testing in Quebec City

Evidence-based design. Reliable delivery.

LEARN MORE

In-situ testing represents a fundamental pillar of geotechnical site characterization in Quebec City, encompassing all field-based investigative methods that evaluate soil, rock, and groundwater properties directly in their natural state. Unlike laboratory tests performed on disturbed samples, in-situ techniques preserve the stress conditions, fabric, and environmental factors that govern how geomaterials actually behave beneath the surface. For a city built upon a complex stratigraphy of glacial deposits, marine clays, and fractured bedrock, this category of investigation is not merely supplementary—it is essential for managing the geotechnical risks inherent to the region's challenging subsurface conditions.

Quebec City's geological framework is dominated by the legacy of the Laurentide Ice Sheet and the post-glacial Champlain Sea, which inundated the St. Lawrence Lowlands approximately 12,000 years ago. This history has left behind thick sequences of sensitive Champlain Sea clays—notorious for their metastable structure and potential for retrogressive landslides—overlain by glacial till and underlain by sedimentary bedrock of the Appalachian and St. Lawrence Platform geological provinces. The presence of these sensitive clays, combined with a shallow water table in many sectors and the fractured nature of the local shale and limestone formations, demands in-situ testing methods that can accurately measure parameters such as undrained shear strength, hydraulic conductivity, and deformation modulus without the disturbance that sampling inevitably introduces.

Demonstration video

Practitioners operating in Quebec must adhere to rigorous national and provincial standards that govern in-situ investigation methods. The Canadian Foundation Engineering Manual (CFEM) provides overarching guidance, while specific test procedures are detailed in CSA and ASTM standards adopted or referenced by Quebec's regulatory framework. The Code de construction du Québec and municipal bylaws, particularly in zones subject to ground movement hazards, often mandate minimum investigation scopes that include specific in-situ tests. For permeability assessments required in environmental or hydrogeological studies, methods such as the field permeability test (Lefranc/Lugeon) are conducted in accordance with standards like ASTM D4630 for rock mass permeability or the procedures outlined in the CFEM for soil testing, ensuring that the data collected meets the scrutiny of regulatory bodies such as the MELCCFP.

The range of projects in the Quebec City region that depend on in-situ testing is extensive. Major infrastructure initiatives—from the ongoing improvements to the Réseau de transport de la Capitale (RTC) transit network to the construction of institutional buildings on the Université Laval campus—rely on cone penetration testing (CPT), vane shear tests, and pressuremeter testing to inform foundation design in the sensitive clay deposits. Dam safety reviews and hydroelectric projects along the Jacques-Cartier and Montmorency river corridors require detailed in-situ permeability assessments using Lugeon and Lefranc tests to evaluate seepage potential and grouting requirements. Residential and commercial developments in landslide-prone sectors such as the Sainte-Foy and Cap-Rouge escarpment areas must incorporate in-situ shear strength measurements to satisfy slope stability analyses required for permitting.

Quick answers

What is the primary advantage of in-situ testing over laboratory testing for Quebec City's sensitive clays?

In-situ testing preserves the natural stress state, moisture content, and microstructure of sensitive Champlain Sea clays, which are highly prone to disturbance during sampling and handling. Laboratory tests on these materials often yield unconservative strength values, whereas field vane shear tests and cone penetration testing provide measurements that better reflect the clay's true undrained shear strength.

Which in-situ testing methods are most commonly required for foundation design in the Quebec City region?

The most frequently specified methods include cone penetration testing (CPT) with pore pressure measurement for stratigraphic profiling and soil classification, field vane shear testing for determining the undrained shear strength of sensitive clays, and pressuremeter testing for obtaining deformation moduli. Standard penetration testing (SPT) remains widely used for correlation purposes and in granular deposits.

How do Quebec's building code requirements influence the scope of an in-situ testing program?

The Code de construction du Québec and related municipal regulations often mandate minimum investigation scopes based on building importance category and site seismicity. In zones designated for landslide risk along the St. Lawrence escarpments, site-specific in-situ shear strength data is typically required to support slope stability analyses, directly influencing the number and type of field tests performed.

What in-situ test is appropriate for measuring rock mass permeability for a grouting program in Quebec City?

The Lugeon test, a type of field permeability test conducted in boreholes within rock formations, is the standard method for assessing rock mass hydraulic conductivity prior to grouting operations. It involves injecting water under pressure into an isolated section of the borehole and measuring the flow rate, providing critical data for designing seepage control measures in fractured limestone or shale.
